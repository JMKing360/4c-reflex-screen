The 4C Reflex Screen

You are not broken.
You have reflexes.

Every person develops automatic patterns. They were useful once — they kept you safe, helped you manage hard situations, helped you survive. The problem is not that you have them. The problem is that they are still running, and most of the time you do not know it.

This screen shows you which of the four reflexes is loudest in you right now — and gives you three specific moves to interrupt it this week. It also answers the questions most people have once they see the pattern: where to start, how to start, and how to keep going.

20 statements. About 6 minutes.

Read each statement. Mark what is actually true — not what you wish were true, not what you think you should answer. The more honest you are, the more useful this becomes.

The scale: Not me · Sometimes me · Often me · This is me.

Complaining

My mind goes to what is wrong before what is working.

I name problems I am not actually working to fix.

I rehearse grievances when I am alone with my thoughts.

I describe my life more than I move it.

Criticizing

The harshest voice in my head often has the final say.

I speak to myself in ways I would not speak to someone I respect.

I rehearse my mistakes longer than I learn from them.

I see my flaws faster than I see what is working.

Comparing

I measure my life against someone else's pace.

I scroll, then feel smaller than before I started.

I downgrade my own progress when I see someone else's.

I lose track of what I want when I see what they have.

Competing

I track who is ahead of me in my field.

My sense of worth is tied to whether I am winning.

I do things mostly to be seen doing them.

I postpone what matters to me for what others will applaud.

Starting & Rhythm

These four questions are about what happens after you decide to change.

I know what I need to do. I just cannot seem to start.

I start well, then lose the rhythm after a few days.

I do not have a clear way to measure whether I am actually growing.

I have no one who will notice — and say something — if I stop.
